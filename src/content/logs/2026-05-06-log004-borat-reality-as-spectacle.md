Borat is not merely a comedy. It functions more like a social experiment disguised as entertainment.

On the surface, it appears as a sequence of exaggerated jokes.  

Yet beneath that lies something more unsettling: how easily reality can be transformed into performance.

Many of the people in the film are unaware that they are part of a narrative.  

They are not acting, and precisely because of that, everything begins to feel like one.

This is where Borat finds its strength.  

It does not create absurdity. It simply allows it to surface on its own.

Certain moments are undeniably funny, but also quietly uncomfortable.  

Not because they are entirely fabricated, but because they feel uncomfortably close to something real.

As a mockumentary, Borat operates on the fragile boundary between fact and construction.  

It does not just question what is real, but who controls how reality is presented.

In the end, what remains is not just laughter,  

but a lingering question:

when reality can so easily become spectacle,  

what remains of authenticity itself?
